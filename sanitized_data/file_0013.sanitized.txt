Market
When are nitrogen service providers called?
There’s a handful of different applications
For large-scale turnarounds, the largest application is catalyst changes. Prepare catalyst reactors for changeout, every 12 months or as long as 7-8 years: we sweep it with our own hydrogen, then plant nitrogen, then we need to turn off our compressor, so we have contaminants and heat in the reactor. We try to drive the temp down with our internal tools, but at some point, we’ll rely on a nitrogen pumper to move large volumes of cold nitrogen to cool the reactor and to purge it of remaining contaminants (LEL, H2S, CO, etc. – any hydrocarbon remaining on the catalyst). Those are large volumes, all at once – we use nitrogen to push it out, and try to cool reactor below 100 degrees so that people can enter it, vacuum it, etc. That’s the bulk of volume we push. Once it’s gas-free, the next step is to dump the catalyst: it could catch on fire, so we keep a nitrogen blanket until all the catalyst is removed from the reactor. Initial purge: lots of volume in a short period of time, probably a 2-4 day window; then another 5-10 days when we remove catalyst little by little, with a nitrogen blanket – lower volume, but continual presence. Then transition to atmosphere, and cut nitrogen. That’s the largest use case for nitrogen at [PERSON] oil refinery for planned events. 
Another use case in turnarounds is pressure-testing, e.g. we need high-pressure units or hydrocrackers. We don’t want to fill it with our own nitrogen, to go faster, in 1 shift instead of multiple days. Then hand over to operations and start the units. That’s the second-biggest use case for turnarounds
Sometimes as part of shutdown process: used to purging out the unit (to speed things up), but case-by-case depend on site needs/practices
Also maintenance of large LPG or gas spheres: hard to get them clear with our internal nitrogen and purging, so pumper trucks prepare the spheres for maintenance. They go 15-20 years before servicing, but take a lot of nitrogen.
Some pipeline applications, if purge-out a line: PIG it or purge it. Interconnecting our facilities or to a storage terminal (feed side)

"When the natural gas feed becomes methane-rich, LNG plant performance shifts in several ways:
Lower LNG Heating Value: A higher methane fraction reduces LNG HHV/Wobbe Index. Product may require Btu adjustment depending on market specifications.
Reduced NGL Recovery: With fewer heavy hydrocarbons, ethane/propane/butane yields drop. This can impact NGL economics and refrigerant makeup availability (if the plant uses internal C2/C3).
Refrigeration System Adjustments: High-methane feed changes the cooling curve and can require rebalancing of mixed-refrigerant components to maintain optimal MCHE performance. Typically involves tuning, not major operating constraints.
Simplified Cryogenic Operation: Lower heavy-hydrocarbon content reduces freezing and condensation risks in the cold box, improving operational stability.
Changes in Boil-Off and Flash Gas Composition: Methane-rich LNG tends to produce slightly higher vaporization rates and leaner flash gas, affecting compressors and fuel systems.
Overall, a methane-rich feed gas does not pose operational risk, but it affects:
LNG product quality (lower BTU)
NGL recovery economics
Refrigeration balance and tuning requirements”
