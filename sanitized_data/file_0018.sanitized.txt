Use Cases
We've honed in on 3 primary use cases for nitrogen services in downstream O&G end-markets (refineries, petrochem, LNG):
Routine maintenance – for example cleaning out tanks / spheres, pigging / cleaning pipelines, etc.
Turnarounds – for instance, refineries purging reactor catalysts and then 'blanketing' equipment with nitrogen to keep it inert
Emergencies – can be small (e.g. nitrogen system is down and needs supplementation) or large (e.g. emergency turnaround because the catalyst needs to be replaced sooner than planned)
Continuous use for operations is focused on gas provision, not services – and seems out of scope / largely not addressable by independents

Based on facility-level spend: # facilities in each end market * addressable spend per facility (broken down by routine maintenance, turnaround, and emergencies)

Competitive Dynamics
 
From our initial research, none of the 3 primary use cases for nitrogen services seem to be “walled off” by large molecule suppliers, and this is unlikely to happen going forward
Even when pipelines or on-site nitrogen generators exist, pumper trucks and pumper truck operators are still required:
“Because you have nitrogen on-site does not mean you don’t need nitrogen services: you need the pumps and the pressure elevation, even if you already have the product” – [ORG], [PERSON]
“Refineries can spend up to $3-5 million a year on pumper-side nitrogen services. This includes refineries with piped-in nitrogen” – [ORG], [PERSON]
"I don’t feel like any part of the market is walled off [by industrial gas providers] [...] I don’t think independents will get marginalized: I think there’s an opportunity for high-quality equipment and good relationships, and local providers" – [ORG], [PERSON] 
"Independents will always be there [because] large providers prefer selling nitrogen to make a margin. [Providing services is] a lot of work and unwanted overhead" – [ORG] Energy Services, [PERSON]

Molecule providers do not view services as a core offering, and are unlikely to increase their focus on services going forward due to operational complexity (TBC)
“You make the money on the service. On the molecule side, gross margins are 30%, compared to 60-70% on services. But services are relatively capital-intensive, with specialized equipment. It takes a lot of specialty equipment that can’t be used other places when the market is down. It’s good to have in your portfolio, but utilization rates are usually low: if you get 30-40% utilization, it’s not bad. It’s also a volatile market: certain services follow the O&G market. When it’s booming, it’s very lucrative and you never have enough assets; but the O&G market is up and down. We would buy used assets, from smaller companies that couldn’t handle the capital requirements. There always seemed to be equipment available, but I don’t know how often companies go bankrupt. When you get a job, many times you’re not the only service provider on-site: maybe because no one has enough equipment, not because customers are trying to mitigate risk. On maintenance side, these can be somewhat emergency calls – so services capture such a premium […] It comes down to having the equipment and the technical people to run the equipment: the product part is the easy part. That stuff is complicated: it’s a combination of pressure and volume, and variability, and maintenance. Typically it’s 24x7: it’s very special people who run those machines. They’re working 24x7 until maintenance is done; the equipment is high pressure and hard to work with […] Services are perceived as a very separate service offering. Operationally, when the “Express Services” [nitrogen service division] guy runs into the room, other colleagues run away – because the other processes that 80% of people are working on are very predictable” – [ORG], [PERSON]
“I don’t feel like services are the focus for the molecule providers: they have nitrogen for them. I haven’t gotten a call from any of the molecule providers in 3 years” – [ORG], [PERSON]
“Large providers prefer selling nitrogen [to independent service providers] and making a margin, compared to putting it in their own pumpers. [The reason is that when you provide services], it requires a lot of overhead and maintenance. There are a lot of systems to take care of in order to keep all that running coherently. They’d rather ship nitrogen in a pipeline or deliver a bottle. They can fill their trucks for free and charge customers; or charge my trucks at a bigger margin, and they don’t need to worry about maintenance, or manpower. With a pumper truck, you need 2-3 people on-site, as well as mechanics” – [ORG] Energy Services, [PERSON]

Customers’ Key Purchasing Criteria vary by situation (turnarounds vs emergencies vs maintenance)
Turnarounds can be planned months ahead of time and are typically bid out to several vendors; cost is usually more important than for emergencies or maintenance.
“If it’s a planned turnaround, my expectation is that sites are bidding it out, looking at past catalyst changes; expecting to use a specific volume over how many days with a specific pump rate. They should do it 8-12 months before the turnaround occurs […] For turnarounds, we really are looking for the lowest bidder qualified to do the work. For new vendors, we’d want some sort of reference, in the [ORG] network, or to another major like Marathon, Phillips, Exxon, or even a letter of recommendation. There’s a reluctancy to trust someone new if they are cheaper” – [ORG], [PERSON]
For emergencies, speed / availability are most important – while cost is a remote concern
“For emergencies, it’s about who answers the phone call and can be there as fast as possible. If the sales rep doesn’t answer the phone for 3 hours, they lost the work. There’s not a lot of discussion on price and equipment. The first call is to whoever they used last: they usually call a specific person – the BD or sales person –, not a 1-800 number” – [ORG], [PERSON]
“In an emergency, you don’t care about money: it’s about who can be reached quickly, and is reliable whenever you call them […] Relationships get developed: you build trust over time. A vendor may get the preference, even if they are a bit expensive” – [ORG], [PERSON]
“In emergency situations, margins are super high, at least double the normal rate. They’re criminal – but that’s because it costs more for the plant to be down than to pay for services” – [ORG], [PERSON]
Maintenance is somewhere in the middle: it is often not planned as thoroughly as turnarounds, and represents lower amounts, so is not bid out as regularly
“Maintenance is between a turnaround and an emergency. It’s planned out 1-3 months before. Only the top planners bid that work. Usually they just make a phone call and get a proposal, because not as expensive, and routine planners aren’t as sophisticated as turnaround planners, and don’t go through the same bidding process or due diligence. They pump out 30 job plans in a week: they’re more interested in speed, like a cashier at the checkout counter. You need to be a rising star planner to bid this out. They usually go to whoever they went to last” – [ORG], [PERSON]
